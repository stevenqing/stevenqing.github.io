I am a Ph.D. student at the Cooperative AI Lab, King's College London, supervised by Dr. Yali Du. My research focuses on building AI agents that can effectively coordinate and cooperate in complex multi-agent environments.

I'm particularly interested in the fundamental question: How can agents learn to cooperate with partners they've never seen before?

Research Interests

  • Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning: Developing algorithms for coordination and cooperation in complex environments.
  • Zero-Shot Coordination: Enabling agents to cooperate with novel partners without prior interaction.
  • Coalition Formation: Understanding when and how agents should form alliances using game-theoretic principles.
  • Causal Inference in MARL: Applying counterfactual reasoning for better credit assignment.

SCHEMER: Skill-Structured Hierarchical Memory for Zero-Shot Embodied Cooperation

Shuqing Shi, [Co-author 1], [Co-author 2], and Yali Du

In Forty-Third International Conference on Machine Learning (ICML), 2026

arXiv Code
How can an embodied agent cooperate with a partner it has never encountered before? We introduce SCHEMER (Skill-struCtured Hierarchical mEmory for zero-shot cooperation), a novel framework that enables zero-shot coordination in embodied multi-agent tasks. Our key insight is to separate abstract skills from their contextualized instances in a hierarchical memory structure. During execution, agents retrieve and adapt relevant skills based on the current context, enabling flexible coordination with novel partners. We evaluate SCHEMER on a suite of cooperative embodied tasks and demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods in zero-shot coordination scenarios.

BRIDGE: Bi-level Reinforcement Learning for Coalition Formation in Multi-Agent Systems

Shuqing Shi, [Co-author 1], [Co-author 2], and Yali Du

In Forty-Third International Conference on Machine Learning (ICML), 2026

arXiv Code
Coalition formation is fundamental to multi-agent cooperation, yet existing approaches typically treat it as a static problem. We propose BRIDGE, a bi-level reinforcement learning framework that jointly learns when to form coalitions (high-level) and how to coordinate within them (low-level). Our approach leverages cooperative game theory to guide coalition value estimation while remaining scalable through learned representations. Experiments on mixed cooperative-competitive environments demonstrate that BRIDGE enables emergent coalition dynamics that outperform both fixed coalition structures and independent learning baselines.

Counterfactual Credit Assignment in Sequential Social Dilemmas

Shuqing Shi, [Co-authors]

In NeurIPS Workshop on Multi-Agent Learning, 2025

PDF Code
Credit assignment in multi-agent systems remains challenging, particularly in social dilemmas where individual and collective interests may conflict. We propose a counterfactual approach that estimates each agent's contribution by reasoning about what would have happened under alternative actions. Our method improves cooperation emergence in sequential social dilemmas while providing interpretable credit assignment.
